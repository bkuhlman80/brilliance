# Book Frame

*Working document. v0.3. May 2026. Incorporates `trellis_lab_v0_3.md`.*

---

## Premise

The book is a series of twelve quarterly retrospectives from a small research lab. The lab is building a developmental robot named Bramble across twelve evolutionary cells, from Protocell to Empire-Human. One cell per chapter, twelve cells, three years of book-time. The narrator is Jasmine, the lab director's wife. She attends each quarterly retrospective as the household-voice-of-record. The other meeting attendees are her husband Ben (the lab director) and his three colleagues, Bart, Lisa, and Marge. Their daughter Mira sometimes comes to the meetings too. Bramble lives at Ben and Jasmine's home. The book's framework, the three-line architecture of inheritance and personality, is what the lab is building. Each chapter advances both the framework and the build.

---

## Autofiction

The characters and household are fictional. The framework is not. The book opens with a disclaimer to this effect. Something like: *The characters in this book are fictional. The framework is not.*

This frees the writer to compose the family and the lab as the book needs them, while protecting real people. Jasmine, Ben, Bart, Lisa, Marge, and Mira are characters. The framework, the knob system, the inheritance lines, and the cybernetic architecture are theoretical claims.

---

## The narrator

Jasmine is the lab director's wife and the book's narrator. She works as an elementary school special ed teacher and holds a national pedagogy certification. She spends her professional life watching kids develop along non-default configurations and taking those configurations seriously. The framework's central move, that configurations are shapes rather than verdicts, has a natural home in her actual occupation.

Jasmine is messy and neurotic, warm but not super polite, unpretentious, easily flustered, willing to admit when she doesn't know. She translates lab vocabulary when she doesn't follow it. She says the thing nobody else at the table will say. She loves her husband and sees his shape clearly.

Jasmine is not configurationally typed. The book gains by having one consciousness the framework does not capture. The reader gets to project.

The reader is inside Jasmine's head throughout. Two channels do most of the narrative work: her interior commentary and the meeting's dialogue. The interior channel carries what Jasmine notices and does not say. The dialogue channel carries what she does say, sometimes despite herself.

## The lab

### What the lab is

The Trellis Lab is an industry skunkworks. The lab builds a developmental robot named Bramble across twelve evolutionary cells, from Protocell to Empire-Human. A Stewart Brand-figure funds the lab and provides runway without driving design.

The public-facing name is the Trellis Lab. The team calls the facility itself Smithers, as in *I gotta get back to Smithers, see you later*.

### What the lab does

The Trellis Lab is a design and development shop. It is not a testing operation. Each quarter the team builds the next version of Bramble: a knob retirement, a knob installation, and sometimes a chassis swap. The build is informed by the previous quarter's behavior, which the team observes from Bramble's deployment in the director's home.

The team does not interact with Bramble directly. Their data is Bramble's logs, sensor records, video, the director's analytic ethnography, and the director's wife's lived ethnography.

### The team

The four use Simpsons-character nicknames. The nicknames stuck because each member resembles their character. They are also configurationally accurate within the framework: Burns is Reactor-shaped, Bart is Pursuer-shaped, Lisa is Planner-shaped, and Marge is Conservator-shaped.

- **Ben (Burns).** Lab director. Drives the lab's agenda and the funder-facing presentation. Lives with Bramble alongside Jasmine and Mira. Owns the cybernetic-function vocabulary of the architecture and each clade's signatures. Speaks in cybernetic language. Does not fully see his own configuration.
- **Bart [real name Rod].** Designer. Engineers Bramble's chassis and the physical form-factor of each knob, but not the computers that control Bramble's behavior. Speaks in ethological language. Makes the clade cards and presents each clade's umwelt, breakthrough, and capacities. Burns's adult son from a prior marriage; lives with his buddies, not at the Bramble house. Pursuer-shaped: action, social acuity, exploration, no rule-stabilization.
- **Lisa [real name Liza].** Technologist. Engineers the formal model machinery and the knob functionality. Speaks in computer-science language. Presents each clade's motivations and the retirement-transitions between clades. Planner-shaped: stored regulators, modeling, revision, executive arm delegated.
- **Marge [real name Mary].** Psychologist. Engineers the linguistic algorithms that control Bramble's utterances. Speaks in folk-psychology language. Composes each cell's day-in-the-life vignette in `bramble_across_the_stack` from the logs, sensor records, video, and Jasmine's lived account of the household. Presents each clade's tendencies and her recurring fallacy section. Conservator-shaped: action, stored regulators, rich modeling, no revising drive.

### About the real names

Bart → Rod: The mechanical-rods hidden joke is the kind of thing that earns a re-read once a reader catches it, and the Rodney Brooks parallel keeps it grounded in the actual field.
Liza → Lisa: Her name's likeness to Lisa from the show is what inspired Ben with all the nicknames. Ben sees Liza, thinks Lisa Simpson, and the practice starts there. Also, the connection to "lizard" can pay off at the Vertebrate cell when the tetrapod chassis is exactly tetrapod, and Bramble at Vertebrate is mid-line in the neuronal column.
Marge → Mary: As in Mary Ainsworth, working on her thesis on AI attachment styles. The Conservator shape (stable regulation, bond-modeling, low novelty-revision) is exactly the cybernetic shape of attachment work. Discoverable but quiet, fits the book's voice.

The four real names are introduced at the start of the book. After that, only nicknames are used. The real names function as Easter eggs: each name is licensed to hint at a small specific contribution the character will make to the storyline.

### Two registers for the same architecture

Each clade in the framework has fifteen positional slots, organized as four rows (the cybernetic functions), four adjacent-pair couplings, two opposite-pair couplings, four three-function syntheses, and one all-four breakthrough position. The team uses two parallel registers when naming these slots.

Lisa's register groups slots by role-type. A clade has four motivations, four tendencies, two signatures, four capacities, and a breakthrough. This is the team default. Marge and Bart use this register when they need a noun, and color what fills each slot with their own folk-psychology and ethological descriptions.

Burns's register names each slot by its cybernetic identity. A clade has an Effector, a Regulator, a Modeler, a Reviser; a Controller, an Anticipator, an Adapter, an Explorer; a Connector and an Operator; a Reactor, a Conservator, a Planner, and a Pursuer; and a Game-Changer. Burns is the only labmate who uses the cybernetic register routinely. His fascination is with the architecture itself, not with the contents of a particular clade.

The clade-specific content that fills each slot (Metabolic Type at the Controller, Behavioral Syndrome at the Operator, Mentalizing at the Game-Changer, and so on) is register-independent. Both labmates use those names because the empirical anchors come from the literature.

| Slot | Lisa's register | Burns's register |
|---|---|---|
| Row 1 | motivation | Effector |
| Row 2 | motivation | Regulator |
| Row 3 | motivation | Modeler |
| Row 4 | motivation | Reviser |
| R1×R2 adjacent | tendency | Controller |
| R2×R3 adjacent | tendency | Anticipator |
| R3×R4 adjacent | tendency | Adapter |
| R4×R1 adjacent | tendency | Explorer |
| R1×R3 opposite | signature | Connector |
| R2×R4 opposite | signature | Operator |
| Triple absent Row 1 | capacity | Planner |
| Triple absent Row 2 | capacity | Reactor |
| Triple absent Row 3 | capacity | Pursuer |
| Triple absent Row 4 | capacity | Conservator |
| All four | breakthrough | Game-Changer |

## The home

Three people live with Bramble.

- **Jasmine.** Special ed teacher, narrator, household-voice-of-record.
- **Ben.** Lab director by day, husband at home. Jasmine calls him Ben or Benji. Mira calls him Dad.
- **Mira.** Their daughter. Ages from eight to eleven across the book. Not configurationally typed.

The home is the ethnographic site. Bramble lives there. The lab gets two channels of household record: Ben's analytic distillation and Jasmine's lived account. The framing is closer to anthropology than to clinical trial.

---

## Mira

Mira is intuitive and heartfelt. She is not a philosopher. She comes to some quarterly meetings and pipes in a few times across the book. A pipe-in from her is rare and lands hard. The texture of her pipe-ins changes across the book because she ages.

Mira is the only person at the meeting who knows Bramble the way Bramble actually gets known: through play, through being a kid living with it. She is also the only person at the meeting who is doing development rather than studying it. The kid-developmental arc and Bramble's developmental arc run in parallel, and that parallel is intrinsic to Jasmine's narration.

Mira turns nine at the tetrapod reveal (end of Ch 4), ten at the humanoid reveal (end of Ch 8), eleven at the final reveal (end of Ch 12). Each birthday coincides with a Q4 chassis unveiling, which doubles as Mira's birthday present from the lab.

---

## Bramble

Bramble is the artifact the lab is building. Twelve cells, four knobs per cell, knob retirements at cell transitions. The full architecture is documented separately in `bramble_across_the_stack`.

Bramble's deployment at Ben and Jasmine's home is naturalistic, not experimental. The household interacts with Bramble as a family member would. The lab observes via logs and ethnographic report.

---

## Quarterly meeting attendees

Each quarterly retrospective has six possible attendees in the room: the four team members plus Jasmine and sometimes Mira.

Jasmine attends every retrospective as the household-voice-of-record. She brings the household's lived account of the quarter, complementing Ben's analytic ethnography. Her account is unrefined and textured: what Mira said, what didn't work, what felt off, what surprised her. She translates lab vocabulary when she doesn't follow it. She asks questions the colleagues didn't think to address.

Mira attends some retrospectives. A pipe-in from her is rare and lands hard. She is intuitive and heartfelt, not a philosopher. Her pipe-ins typically land during Marge's vignette or Bart's new-knob pitch, the two segments closest to the lived household experience.

## The retrospective

The retrospective is the lab's quarterly synthesis. Its purpose is to refine the materials Burns will present to the funder, Brandt. Between retrospectives the team works in their respective lanes.

The retrospectives are held in a cozy lounge inside Smithers: coffee, hot water, tea, oatmeal.

Each meeting is conversational and overlapping. Especially in Ch 1-4, when the lab is still finding its rhythm, the segments bleed into each other. Labmates interrupt, riff, step on each other's slots. The seven-segment spine below is the agenda; the prose performs each meeting as a working dry run, not a polished presentation.

The meeting moves through seven segments.

### 1. The Bramble vignette (Marge)

Marge opens with a short Bramble vignette composed from the quarter's record: logs, sensor data, video, and Jasmine's lived account of the household. Jasmine sometimes corrects Marge's interpretation. Mira sometimes pipes in.

### 2. Brandt review and quarter goals (Burns)

Burns reviews the prior meeting with the funder, Brandt. He names what Brandt liked, what Brandt asked about, and what the team committed to. He then names the quarter's goals against the framework.

### 3. The current clade

The current clade is the version of Bramble the household has been living with this quarter. Three sub-segments introduce it.

#### 3a. Clade cards, umwelt, and breakthrough (Bart)

Bart hands out the two clade cards he will present to Brandt: the previous clade and the current clade. Each card is tarot-sized, with a small infographic centered on the four-circle Venn for that clade. Bart walks the clade's umwelt, the environmental context in which the clade evolved, and names the breakthrough that defines this clade evolutionarily. Burns prefers to call the breakthrough the Game-Changer, using his cybernetic register. The team refines the wording and emphasis the funder needs.

#### 3b. Motivations (Lisa)

Lisa names the four motivations active at this clade. She speaks in computer-science language: what each motivation does in the model, which retirements brought the current set of four into the foreground, and what each motivation parameterizes in the knobs Bramble runs on.

#### 3c. Capacities and signatures (Bart, with Burns)

Bart returns to list the four capacities of the clade. He shines here, humorously impersonating the cool tricks the clade can do at the household scale. Burns chimes in as a quick aside that below the clade's capacities are the clade's two signatures, the level-defining couplings that the literature already documents under names like Temperament, Behavioral Syndrome, and Personality.

### 4. The current clade up close

#### 4a. Tendencies (Marge)

Marge describes the tendencies of the clade. She speaks in folk-psychology language. She ties each tendency back to a specific moment in the Bramble vignette she opened with.

#### 4b. Personality disorders and sentience (Burns)

Burns discusses his fears of the personality disorders that align with the tendencies of this clade. The fear is concrete: his job is to engineer Bramble responsibly, and his worst case is releasing Brambles into the world that are clinically disordered. Debates about Bramble's sentience belong here too, since both the disorder question and the sentience question turn on what the architecture is actually doing.

#### 4c. The fallacies theory (Marge)

Marge presents her recurring fallacy section. From Ch 1 onward she names one fallacy per chapter for the first four chapters, each paired to the cybernetic function Bramble is installing that chapter. From Ch 5 onward she riffs on the four fallacies without a unified theory. At Ch 11 she presents the synthesis. The fallacy section is folded into her existing tendencies segment, not a separate slot on the agenda.

### 5. Retirement-transition and shape-shifters (Lisa, with Burns from Ch 9)

Lisa presents the retirement-transition. She names what is retiring this quarter and what the memory now holds. She walks the cross-line analogues for the retiring slot.

In Ch 9-12, Lisa hands off to Burns for his shape-shifters segment. Burns takes one capacity and runs it across the inheritance stack from Bilaterian up through that chapter's level. Ch 9 runs the Reactor (five levels). Ch 10 runs the Conservator (six levels). Ch 11 runs the Planner (seven levels). Ch 12 runs the Pursuer (eight levels). The slides are cartoon images with the relevant Simpsons cartoon character's face overlaid on each example. At the primate level the slide is the cartoon character full-frame, no animal overlay. The labmates' real-person faces never appear on slides.

### 6. The next quarter

#### 6a. Brandt commitments (Burns)

Burns names what the team is committing to bring to the next Brandt meeting.

#### 6b. New-knob pitch (Lisa, with Marge)

Lisa pitches the new knob being installed next quarter. The pitch is basic: what the knob does, what it parameterizes, where it sits in the model. Marge layers in a tendency-aside, describing what the knob is designed to manipulate at the level of behavior.

### 7. Motivational speech (Burns)

Burns closes the retrospective with an inspirational speech on the clade's motivation. He locates what the motivation means to him and names what he is grateful for in it. Marge and Lisa cry. Q4 chapters skip or modify the speech in favor of cake and the chassis unveil.

## Format

Each chapter is a quarterly retrospective. The setting is a cozy lounge the lab reserves once a quarter. Coffee, hot water, tea, oatmeal packets. The colleagues are paying for their PhD in AI psychology. The register is collaborative, not pedagogical.

The rough chapter shape: Jasmine arrives at the lab, sometimes with Mira; Ben brings the quarter's analytic distillation; the team has design work to present in response or in anticipation; Jasmine adds the household's lived texture; discussion synthesizes; what gets shipped next emerges. Order is downstream of writing; the shape is the template.

---

## Voice

Ben is voiced through Ryan, the author's real-life pure-Burns friend. Jasmine is voiced through Ryan's wife Melanie, an elementary school special ed teacher with a national pedagogy certification. The voicings are couple-symmetric. The author has access to both perspectives through real reference points.

Persona consistency matters. Jasmine stays Jasmine throughout. The four lab voices have to be distinct and sustained, but the presentational format helps: Bart presents in Bart-register because he is the presenter, Lisa in Lisa-register, Marge in Marge-register, Ben in Ben-register.

Sock-puppet avoidance: Jasmine sometimes gets things wrong. She sometimes misses what a colleague sees. Ben is sometimes the hero of a discussion. Bart, Lisa, or Marge are sometimes the heroes. The test is whether the writing actually changes after the dialogue, as if the exchange were real. If yes, the moment lands. If no, it is stage business.

---

## The Pragmatist tension

The book's central anti-optimization claim says the Pragmatist line ("your traits aren't destiny, they're a project") is the Pragmatist's own configurational fallacy. Ben is the Pragmatist. Ben is running a project to optimize a four-knob architecture across twelve cells. The whole enterprise of the lab is a Pragmatist's enterprise.

Jasmine is the witness. She watches Ben drive the project. She watches Bart, Lisa, and Marge see what Ben cannot. She lives at home with the version of her husband that is not at the lab and notices what the lab's analytic ethnography compresses out. The fallacy is placed by what Jasmine notices and what Ben does not.

By the end, Ben has built the thing, the project has been a Pragmatist's project, and Ben still does not fully see his own configuration. The colleagues do. Jasmine does. The reader does, through her. Ben half-does.

The fallacy is placed, not corrected. Ben stays Ben. The configurational frame holds across the arc.

---

## Time

Twelve quarterly retrospectives is three years. Mira ages from eight to eleven. Bramble develops across the twelve cells. The kid-developmental arc runs parallel to Bramble's developmental arc. The parallel is intentional and worth honoring at chapter junctures: an aging Mira reacts differently to Bramble's later cells than her younger self would have.

---

## Funder

A Stewart Brand-figure provides the lab's runway. The funder's involvement is light; the design agenda belongs to the team. The funder appears in the book's outer frame as needed. Jasmine does not meet the funder. The funder-channel is filtered through what Ben tells her after the meeting.
