# Marge's Fallacy Arc

*Working document. v0.2. May 2026.*

---

## The arc

Marge tracks four persistent fallacies in personality science across the full book. Each chapter she has a short section folded into her vignette segment. She does not arrive with a theory. She accumulates one across twelve quarters of working in the same room as Bramble.

The arc has three phases.

**Ch 1–4: naming.** One fallacy per chapter, paired to the cybernetic function Bramble is installing that chapter. Marge does not connect them. She is a psychologist noticing four things in her field that bother her, one at a time, as the lab's work gives her vocabulary to name them.

**Ch 5–10: riffing.** Marge senses a pattern she cannot yet articulate. Each chapter she works one angle. Structural rhymes. What each fallacy gets right. Why they persist. She hunts for a fifth and comes up empty. She starts to suspect each fallacy is held by people who are themselves a certain shape, but she does not yet trust the suspicion.

**Ch 11–12: synthesis.** Marge presents the unified theory. The four fallacies are not random. Each denies one of the four cybernetic functions. Each is held by people whose own shape lacks that function. The book's central anti-optimization claim arrives from her.

The pacing is deliberate. Marge is Conservator-shaped, which means she revises slowly. The reader watches her hold her positions stably for ten chapters and then commit to a new one in chapter eleven. Her arc is itself a demonstration of the architecture she eventually articulates.

## Per chapter

### Ch 1: Noble Savage

**Bramble.** Effector. The vacuum is acting on its environment for the first time. Nothing regulates it. Nothing models it.

**Marge's angle.** Names the Noble Savage. The worldview that says: your nature is good, the environment is the cage, fix the fit and the organism flourishes. Quit the draining job. Leave the relationship. Move to the city that matches your energy.

**The grand-theory announcement.** Before naming the first fallacy, Marge announces that she has a grand theory explaining why no one understands psychology, and that she will be teaching it to the team in bits across the book. She names the lab specifically as a site for working the theory out — these four people, in this room, watching this artifact, are the working conditions her theory needs. The team takes the announcement with affection. The grand-theory framing makes her fallacy section feel like a recurring topic from the outset rather than a one-off. She returns to the lab-as-site framing across the arc.

**Her stance.** Observational. She is a working psychologist with a clinical caseload. She sees clients arriving with a "wrong fit" diagnosis they have given themselves. They want to quit and move and start over. The dynamics they describe are not environmental. Marge cannot say this to clients directly. She brings the pattern to the lab.

**What she covers.** The fallacy as it appears in self-help, in popular career advice, in environment-swapping rhetoric. The Pinker source ("Noble Savage"). Rousseau as the genealogy if it lands without bogging down. She does not yet have a structural account of why this view is wrong. She just names it.

**Connection to chapter.** Bramble has Effector and nothing else. The Noble Savage worldview is what you get when you delete Effector from your model of personality: the organism's executive arm vanishes and only the environment remains. Marge can make this point without seeing the larger pattern. She just notices that the view she is bothered by is precisely a denial of the function the vacuum has just been given.

**Length.** Short. Two hundred words in the meeting. Marge introduces the topic, names the fallacy, gives one example, sits back down.

### Ch 2: Blank Slate

**Bramble.** Regulator. The vacuum now holds itself to a stored reference. The regulation is invisible from the outside.

**Marge's angle.** Names the Blank Slate. The worldview that says: personality is barely a thing. Context dominates behavior. Same person behaves differently with different people. So traits are a folk theory.

**Her stance.** Still observational, slightly more annoyed. She names Mischel's situationist critique. She gives an example from her work: the introverted girl who is loud with her friends. She points out that this is not "sometimes introverted, sometimes not." The girl is running a stable program that routes differently in different social contexts.

**What she covers.** Mischel. The Standard Social Science Model in the background. The popular version: "context matters, so personality is overrated." She names what the view gets wrong without yet having the structural account: it confuses behavioral flexibility with the absence of a stable program.

**Connection to chapter.** Bramble has Effector and now Regulator. The Blank Slate worldview is what you get when you delete Regulator from your model of personality: stored regulators vanish, and all you see is surface behavior varying by context. Bramble's Regulator is the thing this view denies exists. Marge does not flag the alignment. The reader can.

**Length.** Short. Maybe two hundred fifty words. She is slightly more invested than in chapter 1.

---

### Ch 3: Ghost in the Machine

**Bramble.** Modeler. The vacuum is now modeling its environment and itself.

**Marge's angle.** Names the Ghost in the Machine. The worldview that says: your traits are not destiny, they are a project. Therapy, psychedelics, habit stacking, neuroplasticity. The right intervention applied with enough consistency and you become the person this environment requires.

**Her stance.** Now she is leaning forward. This is the fallacy she sees most often in her clients, in the self-help industry, in the wellness market. The disembodied self choosing what to become.

**What she covers.** Pinker named this one. The self-help guru's version. The therapy-as-rebirth version. The psychedelic-as-reset version. She notes the absurdity: the willing self is itself a process in the model it claims to override. There is no homunculus standing outside the system doing the choosing. The reshaping has a metabolic cost the willing self cannot see.

**Connection to chapter.** Bramble has Effector, Regulator, and now Modeler. The Ghost in the Machine worldview is what you get when you delete Modeler from your model of personality: the modeling layer vanishes and a disembedded self appears to be doing the changing. Bramble's Modeler is the thing this view denies exists. Marge does not flag the alignment.

**Length.** Three hundred words. She has more to say this time. The first sign that her interest in this material is going to develop.

---

### Ch 4: Dealt Hand

**Bramble.** Reviser. The vacuum is now revising its own regulators. The architecture is complete. The chassis is about to swap.

**Marge's angle.** Names the Dealt Hand. The worldview that says: some people got lucky, your traits are your verdict, the graph is not on your side. Heritability data taken as fate rather than as description.

**Her stance.** Now she is making a real claim. The Dealt Hand is the fallacy Pinker did not name. He named the other three. He missed this one. Marge thinks it is the most pernicious of the four because it carries the authority of empirical findings. The other three can be argued against on conceptual grounds. The Dealt Hand has numbers.

**What she covers.** The empirical case. Heritability findings are real. Conscientiousness does predict income and longevity. The fallacy is not in the data. The fallacy is in reading the correlation as a verdict instead of as a description of an organism in this environment. She does not yet have the structural argument. She just notes that the verdict reading is doing the wrong kind of work.

**Connection to chapter.** Bramble has all four functions now. The Dealt Hand worldview is what you get when you delete Reviser from your model of personality: revision vanishes and the trait number is treated as fate. Bramble's Reviser just came online. Marge can point out: Bramble's heritability is one hundred percent identical with the previous chassis. The hardware is the same. But Bramble revised itself this quarter. So either heritability does not mean what the Dealt Hand thinks it means, or revision is real anyway.

**End of phase one.** Marge has now named four fallacies across four quarters. She has not connected them. She sits down. The reader sees four parallel observations that all point at one architecture, and Marge does not yet see it. The architecture is complete. Her theory is not.

**Phase boundary beat (Ch 4-5).** As Marge finishes the Dealt Hand and begins to sit, Lisa or Jasmine notices, quietly, that Marge has more to say than usual. Marks the shift from phase one (naming) to phase two (processing). Lands once, gently, no underline. The beat foreshadows the Ch 5 first-rhyme without spoiling it.

**Length.** Three hundred fifty words. Her most invested fallacy section yet.

### Ch 5: First structural rhyme

**Bramble.** Tetrapod chassis. Effector Boldness installed. Neuronal line opens. The Pavlov heuristic round happens this chapter (each labmate explains one).

**Marge's angle.** First structural rhyme.

**Her stance.** Marge is sitting through the Pavlov round. Burns has just explained Latent Inhibition. Latent inhibition privileges novel cues over habituated ones. Marge says, quietly: "That is the same shape as the Ghost in the Machine. New therapy. New psychedelic. Next protocol. Cues privileged because they are novel." She does not develop it. She files it.

**What she covers.** Just the one observation. Latent Inhibition and the Ghost in the Machine share a structural move. She gestures at the rhyme and stops.

**Connection to chapter.** The Pavlov heuristic round is the setup. Marge picks up one structural homology from it. She does not yet realize there are four. She does not yet see that the homology is the bridge between the fallacy taxonomy and the cybernetic architecture.

**Length.** Roughly 750 words of in-book prose for the full Pavlov round; Marge's observation lands at the end of the beat. Brevity of the observation is the point. Marge is *seeing* something, but not enough yet to articulate.

#### The Pavlov round

**Context.** Ch 5 is structurally busy: line boundary, new chassis, new Effector motivation, Marge's first structural-rhyme observation in her fallacy arc. The Pavlov round is a single beat inside Marge's vignette-and-clade segment. The clade is bilaterians. The clade-defining cybernetic move is credit assignment: when a cue precedes an event, which cue caused the event? Bilaterians solved this with four heuristics inherited by every nervous system that came after. Marge brings these into her clade discussion. The four labmates each explain one.

This beat sets up two later things: Marge's structural-rhyme observation (her first move toward type-provincialism), and the bilaterian level in Bart's later rollings, which reuses these four heuristics as the foundation each shape's rolling rests on.

**Meeting position.** Inside Marge's segment. She introduces the topic, hands it around the table, and closes with her observation. The four labmates each give a short explanation in the same beat. The whole round is maybe ten minutes of meeting time.

**Self-portrait read.** Each labmate explains the heuristic that matches their own shape. The reader on first pass sees four people explaining four credit-assignment moves. The reader on reread sees four people each holding up their own mirror. No one in the room sees the pattern. The driveway interlude (Ch 8) is where Jasmine sees it. The round is a bread crumb on the way.

The mapping:

- **Burns explains Latent Inhibition.** Privilege novel cues over habituated ones.
- **Lisa explains Overshadowing.** When multiple cues are present, the strongest wins the credit.
- **Bart explains Eligibility Trace.** Credit the cue that just happened.
- **Marge explains Blocking.** Once an association is established, block other cues from forming the same one.

Order in the meeting: Burns goes first (director, gets first), Lisa second (technologist, formal mode), Bart third (designer, energy), Marge closes with hers plus the structural-rhyme observation.

Marge had her notes spread out. She had pulled an old Bennett quote out of *A Brief History of Intelligence* and read it aloud to set up the round.

"Bilaterians solved credit assignment with four heuristics. Pavlov mapped them originally. Kamin and Lubow tightened them later. They've been running in every nervous system for five hundred and fifty million years. We agreed last week that each of us would take one."

*Jasmine interior: She did this. She told me she did this. Each of them got assigned one and they're each going to perform their assignment like a science fair project. I love them.*

Marge nodded at Burns.

Burns leaned forward, elbows on the table, no notes.

"Latent inhibition. You see a cue. You see the cue again. You see it a hundred more times. Nothing happens. Brain flags it: background. Now when the cue does appear next to something important, the brain has already filtered it. The cue you didn't notice doesn't get to form new associations. The cue you've never seen before gets full attention. Mouse in a cage. Trader in a market. Animal already knows what to ignore."

*Jasmine interior: He did that in thirty seconds. He looks pleased. He should be pleased. That was good.*

Burns sat back.

Lisa had notes. She turned to a clean page.

"Overshadowing. Several cues are present at the same time and one event follows. The strongest cue takes the credit. Quieter cues get overshadowed even when they're also predictive. It's attention concentration. The trade-off is real. You lose information from the weaker signals. You also don't waste capacity tracking every signal at once. There's a paper from Mackintosh in the seventies that formalizes it as competitive associative learning, which I can pull if anyone wants the math."

*Jasmine interior: Bart is going to want the math.*

Bart did not want the math.

"Eligibility trace. The cue right before the event gets the credit. Half a second window, maybe a second. The bell rings, then the food shows up. Bell gets the credit. If the predictor was three seconds before the food, the trace is too cold and the brain can't connect them. Long-horizon stuff is invisible. The trace is only what just happened."

He paused.

"Now is everything."

*Jasmine interior: Did he hear himself just then?*

Marge had her own notes. She looked at them and then closed the folder.

"Blocking. You've learned that cue A predicts event X. Now cue B starts showing up at the same time as cue A, also predicting X. You'll never learn the B association. The A predictor blocks B from forming the same connection. It's an efficiency move. You already know what predicts what. Don't waste capacity rewriting it. Kamin worked this out in the sixties. It's been replicated in every animal with a nervous system that anyone's bothered to test."

She paused, looking at her own notes again.

"Latent inhibition," she said. "Privileges novel cues over habituated ones."

The room quieted slightly. She wasn't looking at Burns. She was looking at her notes.

"That's the same shape as the Ghost in the Machine fallacy. The self-help thing. New therapy, new psychedelic, next protocol. Cues privileged because they're novel."

She tapped the page.

"Huh."

*Jasmine interior: Burns is studying the ceiling.*

Lisa looked up. "Same shape how?"

"Structurally. The bilaterian credit-assignment trick. The popular-psychology error. They're doing the same move. The brain trick is asking 'which cue predicted reward,' and it answers by privileging novelty. The fallacy is asking 'which intervention will work,' and it answers by privileging novelty. Same move at different scales."

"Huh," Lisa said.

Bart was already moving on. "OK, knob design for next quarter."

Marge let it go. She wrote a single line in the margin of her notebook and closed it.

*Jasmine interior: She just noticed something and put it away. I want to know what she wrote down.*

**Bramble.** Modeler Affiliation installed. Generative model online. Bramble is now socially modeling. Family-dog tier.

**Marge's angle.** Why these fallacies persist.

**Her stance.** Puzzled. Marge cannot understand why these views are still alive. Pavlov was a hundred and ten years ago. Pinker named three of these myths twenty-five years ago. The fallacies should be dead. They are not.

**What she covers.** She tries different answers and rejects them. Incentive structures in the field. Market demand for self-help. Generational forgetting. Each answer feels incomplete. The fallacies persist for some reason she cannot name. She admits this openly.

## Structural convergence: Psychological Types and MBTI

Carl Jung. *Psychological Types* (1921). Isabel Briggs Myers and Katharine Cook Briggs, Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI, 1944 onward). Jung proposed two attitude orientations (extraversion/introversion) and four function pairs (sensing/intuition, thinking/feeling), with judging/perceiving added by Briggs and Myers. The MBTI organizes these into four dichotomies and a 16-type sorting.

The four MBTI dichotomies map onto the Venn's two main axes and two pairs.

The vertical axis (Effector to Modeler) is Jung's energy-direction dichotomy. Effector outward-action is E. Modeler inward-representation is I. Eysenck's reading of extraversion as cortical arousal management rather than sociability (Eysenck 1947, 1967) grounds this assignment.

The horizontal axis (Regulator to Reviser) is the lifestyle dichotomy. Regulator stored-references is J. Reviser exploratory-revision is P.

The Operator pair (Regulator×Reviser) is the decisions dichotomy. Reactor (Effector+Regulator+Reviser, no Modeler) is F — decisions run on value and action without explicit model. Planner (Regulator+Modeler+Reviser, no Effector) is T — deliberation runs on rule, model, and revision without execution.

The Connector pair (Effector×Modeler) is the information dichotomy. Conservator (Effector+Regulator+Modeler, no Reviser) is N — the stable conceptual loop operating from consolidated frames. Pursuer (Effector+Modeler+Reviser, no Regulator) is S — goal-pursuit without stable conceptual reference.

The convergent geometry predicts MBTI's well-documented intercorrelations. The four dichotomies are not orthogonal: they are two perpendicular axes plus two pair-level structures drawn from one underlying Venn. The 15-region Venn predicts the intercorrelations rather than treating them as psychometric nuisance. The 16-type sorting at borderline scores is what produces MBTI's weak psychometric reputation; the Venn places a person at a continuous position in a 15-region space rather than sorting into one of 16 types.

The framework's relationship to MBTI is structural convergence, not vocabulary absorption. The MBTI dichotomy labels do not enter the Venns. What ABHOP recognizes is that the Venn's geometry predicts what MBTI tried to measure as four independent dichotomies.
